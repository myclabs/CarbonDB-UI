About

CarbonDB is a life cycle assessment (LCA) open dataset centered on greenhouse gas and energy impacts.

It is structured as a domain ontology in terms of the Web Ontology Language (OWL 2) W3C recommendation.

Source data is maintained by My C-Sense as an open source project, from where it can be freely downloaded, consulted, and edited using any ontology editor, for instance Protégé.

Changes on ontology can be followed here. Please feel free to contribute by submitting issues or directly submitting an improved version of the ontology.

Secondary data (computed data like elementary flows) are derived from source data (and still expressed in RDF) by an inference engine maintained by My C-Sense as an open source project.

The remaining layer is the user interface application displaying the data. Only this part of the project has not been made open-source/data yet.

This project benefited from research jointly carried on by My C-Sense and computer science laboratory LIRIS from Université de Lyon. Its main innovative feature is the fact that dependency relations among processes can be defined at a macro-level, which makes the model easier to understand and maintain.

Help

CarbonDB is based on classical life cycle assessment concepts. The basic objects involved are processes, relations, coefficients, elementary flows, impacts.

  • Processes represent any kind of product, service, human activity, or even physical process. Processes are related by dependency relations ("economic flows") and associated to elementary flows to / from biosphere.
  • Coefficients are just scalars, they play the role of weights in dependency relations between processes (for this reason they are called "coefficients").
  • Impacts of a process are computed from its elementary flows (in general through a simple linear combination). For instance, for a given process, adding its greenhouse gas emissions weighted by global warming potentials of corresponding gases on a definite duration (say 100 years), one obtains a single figure (impact) that sums up those emissions.

Here are some features of the contents.

  • Processes and coefficients are grouped in groups of processes and groups of coefficients. It is those groups that appear as clickable links in the tree displayed in tree page. Their names are preceded by "[P]" or "[C]" in order to distinguish between these two concepts.
  • Dependency relations ("economic flows") are defined at a "macro" level between groups of processes, and weighted by groups of coefficients. On the page of any group of processes or coefficients, you will find the relations in which this group is involved. Sometimes, weights have to be used as denominators (in this case they are displayed with a "-1" exponent in this view).
  • From these source "macro" relations, "micro" relations (the usual "economic flows") are derived through an algorithm which is quite intuitive but not so easy to explain in a few words. This paper explains in details how this stuff works, althougr we hope its reading is not required for understanding and using the model in practice.
  • Elementary flows and impacts are of any kind, and can be entirely customized in the model. At this stage elementary flows and impacts deal with: greenhouse gas emissions, primary energy extraction, and final energy consumption, but other kinds of elementary flows and impacts could be added in the future.
  • In the view of a process group, the default displayed value type is the total of greenhouse gas emissions, expressed in 100 years GWP CO2-equivalent. But other impacts (like total of greenhouse gas emissions expressed in 20 years GWP CO2-equivalent) and all elementary flows (like emissions of greenhouse gas for a particular gas or primary energy extraction for a particular source of primary energy) are available through a scrolling list.
  • All source data are carried out by coefficients. In other words, all elementary flows are calculated from a very short list of purely physical processes (like direct emissions of greenhouse gases) through dependency relations and coefficient values. All these processes are contained in category "Miscellaneous > Direct generic elementary flows".
  • Values carry no uncertainty at this stage, but those will be added as soon as the values of uncertainties on source data will be chosen.
  • In dependency relations, units should be compatible only at the physical quantity level (unit conversions are carried out automatically in the calculation of elementary flows).

You can browse the data either by:

  • unfolding the categories on tree page,
  • or, accessing to groups of processes or coefficients from the graph page,
  • or, accessing to groups from their references, starting from the reference page,
  • or, once you picked up a group of processes or coefficients, clicking on other groups involved in neighboring relations.

Two versions of the data are available to users: a "last stable version" (default choice, labelled with a tag number) and a "work in progress" version (which may more likely display inconsistencies).

Units and unit conversions are addressed by My C-sense Units application.

Have fun!

Forthcoming functionalities

By order of release:

  • Ability to consult values converted in any unit.
  • Uncertainties on values of source and computed data.
  • Webservices access.
